## Overview

Tumor growth depends upon cancer cells acquiring nutrients from their environment and using these molecules to fuel proliferation. Our laboratory investigates the nature and regulation of **metabolic adaptation during tumorigenesis and metastasis**, with the goal of identifying therapeutic vulnerabilities in cancer cells.

We are especially interested in the biochemical processes underlying nutrient sensing and metabolic/redox homeostasis, including regulation of the protein post-translational modification landscape by reactive metabolites.

---

<div class="research-section" id="metabolic-reprogramming">

<h2><span class="icon"><i class="fas fa-flask"></i></span> Metabolic Reprogramming in Cancer</h2>

Cancer cells undergo dramatic metabolic reprogramming to support their rapid proliferation and survival. Our lab investigates how oncogenes and tumor suppressors rewire cellular metabolism, focusing on:

- **Altered nutrient acquisition** - How cancer cells increase uptake of glucose, glutamine, and other nutrients
- **Biosynthetic pathway activation** - Understanding upregulation of pathways that produce building blocks for cell growth
- **Metabolic enzyme regulation** - Studying how key metabolic enzymes are controlled at transcriptional and post-translational levels

We use a combination of metabolomics, biochemistry, and genetic approaches to understand these processes and identify potential therapeutic targets.

</div>

---

<div class="research-section" id="nutrient-sensing">

<h2><span class="icon"><i class="fas fa-dna"></i></span> Nutrient Sensing & Redox Homeostasis</h2>

Cancer cells must continuously sense and respond to nutrient availability in the tumor microenvironment. We study:

- **Glutamine metabolism** - Our work has shown that cancer cells adapt to glutamine starvation by activating alternative pathways to maintain alpha-ketoglutarate levels
- **Serine/glycine metabolism** - Investigating the role of PHGDH and related enzymes in supporting cancer cell survival
- **Redox balance** - Understanding how cancer cells maintain redox homeostasis through metabolic adaptations

A key finding from our lab demonstrated that simultaneously targeting glutamine metabolism and the PHGDH pathway can effectively starve breast cancer cells by blocking both their primary nutrient source and backup supply.

</div>

---

<div class="research-section" id="metastasis">

<h2><span class="icon"><i class="fas fa-project-diagram"></i></span> Metastasis & Tumor-Host Interactions</h2>

Metastatic spread is the primary cause of cancer mortality. We investigate the metabolic adaptations required for cancer cells to colonize distant organs, with a particular focus on:

- **Breast cancer brain metastasis** - Understanding how breast cancer cells adapt their metabolism to survive in the brain microenvironment
- **Tumor-host metabolic crosstalk** - Studying how tumors interact with and reprogram host metabolism
- **Immune-metabolic interactions** - Investigating connections between tumor metabolism and anti-tumor immune responses

Our goal is to identify metabolic vulnerabilities that are specific to metastatic cells and can be targeted therapeutically.

</div>
